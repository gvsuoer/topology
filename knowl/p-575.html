If it took three operations to transform “green” into “grease”, we could say that the distance between “green” and “grease” is at most 3. However, it may be possible to transform “green” into “grease” in fewer than 3 operations, which might change our opinion of the distance between these words. In general, to define the Levenshtein distance \(d_L\) between a sting \(x\) and a string \(y\text{,}\) let \(m_d\) denote the number of deletions, \(m_i\) the number of insertions, and \(m_s\) the number of substitutions we use to get from \(x\) to \(y\text{.}\) There may be many different combinations of \(m_d\text{,}\) \(m_i\text{,}\) and \(m_s\) that get us from \(x\) to \(y\text{,}\) so we want the smallest number.
Definition 4.2.
The Levenshtein distance \(d_L(x,y)\) between strings \(x\) and \(y\) is
\begin{equation*} d_L(x,y) = \min\{m_d+m_i+m_s\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Prove that the Levenshtein distance function is really a metric on the set of all possible words (sensical or nonsensical).
