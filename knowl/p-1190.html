Let \(X\) be a metric space and \(A\) a subset of \(X\text{.}\)
  1. A point \(x \in X\) is a boundary point of \(A\) if every neighborhood of \(x\) contains a point in \(A\) and a point in \(X \setminus A\text{.}\)
  2. A point \(x\) is a limit point of \(A\) if every neighborhood of \(x\) contains a point in \(A\) different from \(x\text{.}\)
  3. A point \(a \in A\) is an isolated point of \(A\) if there is a neighborhood \(N\) of \(a\) such that \(N \cap A = \{a\}\text{.}\)

Also, \(0.5\) is a limit point but neither a boundary or isolated point. With \(A\) as subset of \(\R\) with the discrete metric, every point of \(A\) is an isolated point but no point in \(\R\) is a boundary point or a limit point of \(A\text{.}\) So even though every boundary point is either a limit point or an isolated point, the three concepts are different.
