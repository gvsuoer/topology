There is no reason to restrict ourselves to a Cartesian product of just two sets. This is an idea that we have encountered before. The Cartesian product \(\R \times \R\) is the standard real plane that we denote as \(\R^2\) and the Cartesian product \(\R \times \R \times \R\) is the three-dimensional real space denoted as \(\R^3\text{.}\) If we have an indexed collection \(\{X_{i}\}\) of sets, with \(i\) running through the set of positive integers, then we can define the Cartesian product of the sets \(X_{i}\) as the set of infinite sequences \((x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n, \ldots)\text{,}\) where \(x_i \in X_i\) for each \(i \in \Z^+\text{.}\) We denote this cartesian product as
\begin{equation*} \Pi_{i \in \Z^+} X_i = \Pi_{i=1}^{\infty} X_i\text{.} \end{equation*}
