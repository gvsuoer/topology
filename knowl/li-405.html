Item 3.

Let \(A\) and \(B\) be nonempty subsets of \(\R\) that are bounded above and below. Let \(A+B = \{a+b \mid a \in A, b \in B\}\text{.}\)
  • Follow the steps below to show that \(\sup(A+B) = \sup(A) + \sup(B)\text{.}\)
    1. Let \(x = \sup(A)\) and \(y = \sup(B)\text{.}\) Show that \(x+y\) is an upper bound for \(A+B\text{.}\)
    2. The previous part shows that \(A+B\) is bounded above and so has a supremum. Let \(z = \sup(A+B)\text{.}\) Explain why \(z \leq x+y\text{.}\)
    3. To show that \(z = x+y\) we have to prove that \(z\) cannot be strictly less than \(x+y\text{.}\) Suppose to the contrary that \(z \lt x+y\text{.}\) Let \(\epsilon = x+y-z\text{.}\) Use the result of ExerciseÂ 2 to arrive at a contradiction.
  • Prove that \(\inf(A+B) = \inf(A) + \inf(B)\text{.}\)
  • Prove or disprove: \(\sup(A \cup B) = \max\{\sup(A), \sup(B)\}\)
  • Prove or disprove: \(\inf(A \cup B) = \min\{\inf(A), \inf(B)\}\)
