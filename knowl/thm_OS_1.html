Theorem 8.3.

Let \(X\) be a metric space. A subset \(O\) of \(X\) is open if and only if \(O\) is a union of open balls.

Proof.

Let \(X\) be a metric space and \(O\) a subset of \(X\text{.}\) To prove this biconditional statement we first assume that \(O\) is an open set and demonstrate that \(O\) is a union of open balls. Let \(a \in O\text{.}\) Since \(O\) is open, there exists \(\epsilon_a \gt 0\) so that \(B(a, \epsilon_a) \subseteq O\text{.}\) We will show that
\begin{equation*} O = \bigcup_{a \in O} B(a, \epsilon_a)\text{.} \end{equation*}
By the way we chose \(\epsilon_a\text{,}\) \(B(a, \epsilon_a) \subseteq O\) for every \(a \in O\text{.}\) So \(\bigcup_{a \in O} B(a, \epsilon_a) \subseteq O\text{.}\) For the reverse containment, let \(x \in O\text{.}\) Then \(x \in B(x, \epsilon_x)\) and so \(x \in \bigcup_{a \in O} B(a, \epsilon_a)\text{.}\) Thus, \(O \subseteq \bigcup_{a \in O} B(a, \epsilon_a)\text{.}\) We conclude that \(O\) is a union of open balls if \(O\) is an open set.
The proof of the converse is left for the following activity.
