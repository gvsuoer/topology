A natural start to building a digital topology might be to identify neighborhoods. The idea of a neighborhood is to consider elements that are close to a point, and in the digital world there are different ways to do this. Given a point \((x,y)\) in \(\Z^2\text{,}\) the 4-neighbors of \((x,y)\) are the points vertically or horizontally adjacent to \((x,y)\text{:}\) that is, the points \((x \pm 1, y)\) and \((x, y \pm 1)\text{.}\) The 8-neighbors of \((x,y)\) are the 4-neighbors along with the points diagonally adjacent to \((x,y)\text{:}\) that is, \((x \pm 1, y)\text{,}\) \((x, y \pm 1)\text{,}\) \((x \pm 1, y \pm 1)\text{.}\) These neighbors are illustrated in FigureÂ 20.10, with the crosses indicating the neighbors of the highlighted point.
