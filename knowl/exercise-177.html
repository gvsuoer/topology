A property of a topological space is said to be hereditary if that property is inherited by every subspace. We state this more formally in the following definition.

Definition 15.3.

A property \(P\) of a topological space \(X\) is hereditary if every subspace of \(X\) also has property \(P\text{.}\)
Show that properties \(T_1\text{,}\) \(T_2\text{,}\) and \(T_3\) are hereditary. (The separation axioms \(T_i\) are found in ChapterÂ 13.) The fact that \(T_4\) is not hereditary is somewhat difficult. One example is the Tychonoff plank (which is normal) with the Deleted Tychonoff plank (which is not normal) as subspace. An interested reader can consult Counterexamples in Topology (2nd ed.), Lynn Arthur Steen and J. Arthur Seebach, Jr., Dover Publications, 1978.
