A sequence \((x_n)\) in a metric space \((X,d)\) is a Cauchy sequence if given \(\epsilon \gt 0\) there exists \(N \in \Z^+\) such that \(d(x_n, x_m) \lt \epsilon\) whenever \(n, m \geq N\text{.}\) Every convergent sequence is a Cauchy sequence. A metric space \(X\) is said to be complete if every Cauchy sequence in \(X\) converges to an element in \(X\text{.}\) For example, \((\R, d_E)\) is complete while \((\Q, d_E)\) is not. Although we will not prove it, the metric space \((\mathcal{H}(\R^2), h)\) is complete. Show that the sequence \((S_n)\) is a Cauchy sequence in \(\mathcal{H}(\R^2)\text{.}\) The limit of this sequence is the famous Sierpinski triangle.
