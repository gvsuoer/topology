Now suppose that \(X_1\text{,}\) \(X_2\text{,}\) and \(Y\) are topological spaces, and that \(f: Y \to X_1 \times X_2\) is a function. Then \(\pi_1 \circ f\) maps \(Y\) to \(X_1\) and \(\pi_2 \circ f\) maps \(Y\) to \(X_2\text{.}\) Since the composition of continuous functions is continuous, we can see that if \(f\) is continuous so are \(\pi_1\circ f\) and \(\pi_2 \circ f\text{.}\) To determine if \(f\) is a continuous function, it would be useful to know if the converse is true. A key idea in the proof is the result of ExerciseÂ 9 that if \(R\text{,}\) \(S\text{,}\) and \(T\) are sets, and \(g: R \to S\) and \(h : S \to T\) are functions, then \((h \circ g)^{-1}(O) = g^{-1}(h^{-1}(O)\) for any subset \(O\) of \(T\text{.}\)
